Ultralearning

Back in September 2022, Audible was giving out free 3-month membership trials. As an casual podcast listener, I thought audiobooks would be a great time filler while commuting to, from, and between classes on campus. As an Audible member, you receive 1 credit every month, which allows you to buy and download one audiobook of your choice (I swear this is not a paid promotion, you'll see why I'm explaining this soon). I ended up not using the app as often as I thought I would, so I cancelled the subscription a few weeks before it expired. But before I did that, I spent the two credits that I had accumulated with my membership to get some free audiobooks. One of the audiobooks I downloaded was Ultralearning by Scott Young.

This book had me completely hooked. Young (the author) rose to fame after completing what he called the 'MIT Challenge,' self-learning MIT's 4-year Bachelor of Computer Science curriculum and passing all the final tests within a year. In Ultralearning, he discusses the methods and paradigms he, and other ultralearners like him, employed to achieve ambitious goals through aggressive, self-directed learning.

It's safe to say that I won't be dropping out of university in favour of such an approach. I'm fortunate enough to have the financial resources to attend university, and I'm thoroughly enjoying it so far. However, I was still eager to give Ultralearning a shot—I was already using some of the tips and tricks mentioned in the book, but there were many more pieces of advice that I have never even heard of before.

The winter break of 2022/23 presented itself as a natural opportunity for an inital foray in ultralearning. I had two weeks with minimal distractions, and a clear, pressing, mission that I needed to accomplish anyways (preparing for technical coding interviews). Below, I have documented the lessons learned from this two week DIY pseudo-boot bamp, and hope to add to this page as other ultralearning opportunities presents themselves to me.
