The Art of Thinking Clearly

Rolf Dobelli

Rating: 7.5/10.0

99 chapters, each one describes a mental fallacy. This book is worth reading because our “squishy human brains” (as Professor Gregor Richards would say) are not designed for the modern world. For hundreds of thousands of years, our brains evolved to fit the needs of hunter-gatherers. Then 10,000 years ago we started farming. And now we have cruise missiles and TikTok rizz parties. So it’s probably a good idea to try and better equip our brains to deal with the present day.

I don’t have much else to say. Here are some interesting (kind of) contradictory fallacies. Just some food for thought:

  • Gambler’s fallacy: The roulette was red 5 times in a row now, it must be black this time!
  • Hot hand fallacy: The stock market keeps going up. It must go up again this year!

  • Action Bias: We’d rather do anything than sit and wait in the face of uncertainty (soccer goalies almost always dive left or right, even though a third of penalties are kicked in the middle)
  • Omission Bias: We prefer inaction when it comes to certain, negative outcomes (it’s easier for referees to not call a foul than to call a wrong one, especially when it’s game altering)

  • Primacy effect: First impressions have an outsized effect on immediate-term decisions
  • Recency effect: Last impressions have a bigger effect over longer periods of time
