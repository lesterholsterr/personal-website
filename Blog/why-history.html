Why YOU should study history

26 JUN 2024

1. Do it for yourself

If you’re around my age, your natural instinct before learning something is to ask: “Is it gonna be on the test?” Because if it’s not, then that thing is definitely not being learned. And unless you’re a history major, this stuff is not going to be tested, right? Wrong. There is a test. Here’s a sneak peek at some of the questions:

  • Question 1: Will you be easily swayed by empty rhetoric every time you turn on the news?
  • Question 2: Do you understand the origins of your government, economy, society, and the problems facing them every time you step into a voting booth?
  • Question 3: Can you understand the experiences of people who grew up in countries other than this one?
  • Question 4: Can you place the events of your life in a broader context to make more informed decisions?
  • Question 5: Will you have the foresight to live in a way that you can look back on and be proud of?

You will be tested on first dates, job interviews, long bus rides, and more. Nobody’s going to ask you what year the Magna Carta was signed (answer: 1215) or who your favourite enlightenment era philosopher is (I like Rousseau), but you will be expected to know your rights and responsibilities as a citizen of a liberal democracy. And you will be expected to defend yourself, your family, and your friends if rights are infringed. If that doesn’t sound important enough, you will also be expected to answer your kids’ and grandkids’ questions when they ask you about what it was like to live in the early 2000s. So yes, there is a test.

2. Do it for others

“That’s great and all, but I’m studying computer science/engineering/pre-med/<insert your major here> and I’m way too busy for this.” Fair point, but consider the following Dr. Seuss quote:

“Today you are you, that is truer than true. There is no one alive who is youer than you.”

Your lived experiences are unique. They are the building blocks of your beliefs and values and identity. And that’s great, I celebrate that. But you’re still a dumbass. And so am I. We are all going through life making decisions based on a sample size of one life. There’s a pretty big margin of error there.

I’m not saying you should study history to not repeat the mistakes of the past — I always thought that was a somewhat silly argument. Like great, now I know not to invade Russia in the winter? That will surely come in handy if I ever command an army. What I am saying is that by learning about the experiences of others, you can begin to think more complexly about your fellow human beings. I will never know what it is like to be you, but at least I can give it my best shot. And if we all give it our best shot, I think we can build deeper and more meaningful relationships. Yeah, you’re busy with school or work. But how would you rather be remembered: As a wealthy person with a successful career? Or as a caring person who left their community a little better than they found it?

3. Do it for the world

Why is it that the good guys win every single war? We all know that George Washington bravely fought for and secured America’s independence. But if Britain put the revolution down, don’t you think we would be learning about how a few pesky disgruntled Americans led by a silly man with wooden teeth started a failed rebellion? And if the Confederates won the Civil War, wouldn’t we be learning about how the Southerners heroically prevailed against the tyrannical Union that tried to take away our right to own slaves? Look, I’m not trying to defend slavery. The point is that history is written by the victors, and I don’t think that’s always a good thing. To quote Robert F. Kennedy Jr.,

“Are we going to remain stuck in a self-righteous story in which America is categorically good, and our opponents are irredeemably evil?”

When we only hear one side of the story, everything becomes a struggle between us and them, between good and evil, Ukraine and Russia, Israel and Palestine, India and Pakistan, Shia and Sunni. And need I mention the culture wars we face domestically? The solution, in my opinion, is history. History is just a collection of stories. In 2024, what drives conflicts is not a lack of resources; there is enough to go around. What drives conflicts are the stories we tell each other. And if we all become avid students of history instead of blindly accepting the narrative that we hear the most frequently, then maybe, just maybe, we can write a happier ending for the story of us.

That’s why YOU should study history.
